Nobody taught them to do any of this.

There is no goal, no score, and no reward anywhere in Vivarium. Each creature just runs a small neural network it inherited from its parent. The ones whose networks happen to steer them toward food live long enough to reproduce — passing on those networks, slightly mutated. Everything that looks like purpose — foraging, fleeing, hunting, migrating, learning — is natural selection quietly editing random behaviour into competent behaviour, right in front of you. Fitness isn't a number the program computes. Fitness is just: did you survive long enough to reproduce?

What's cool about it

🧭

Purpose from physics

No fitness function. Behaviour that looks designed is selection in disguise — the single idea the whole project is built to show.

🔺

A food web that evolves

A single diet gene lets predators and prey diverge from a common ancestor. In some worlds a full predator–prey arms race ignites — and nobody scripted the hunters.

🌳

A living tree of life

Creatures are grouped into species by genetic similarity and drawn as a Muller plot — watch lineages branch, sweep to dominance, and go extinct in real time.

🧠

Brains that learn

Turn on neural plasticity and brains adapt within a lifetime — and since it starts at zero, a lineage only learns if evolution discovers that learning pays (the Baldwin effect).

🧬

Brains that grow

With evolvable topology (NEAT), brains start as bare graphs and grow their own structure — new connections, new neurons — only where it earns its keep. Click a creature to see its network.

🌍

A world that never settles

Seasons swell and starve the food; biomes drift across the map; the dead feed the living. Where and when a creature lives both matter.

How it works

Every creature carries a genome — the weights of its neural-network brain, plus a few genes for its body (size, metabolism, colour, diet). Each tick, every creature runs the same tiny loop:

  1. Sense — build an input vector: the direction and closeness of the nearest food, the nearest creature it could eat, and the nearest one that could eat it; its own energy, an internal clock, and more.
  2. Think — run those inputs through its neural network.
  3. Act — turn and thrust from the network's outputs, then pay an energy cost for moving and merely existing.
  4. Live & die — eat to refill energy; cross a threshold and reproduce a mutated copy of yourself; run out of energy, or grow too old, and die.

That's the entire rulebook. It's fully deterministic — a given seed replays the exact same world every time, which is why any world you find is a shareable link. Curious about the details? Read the science or the architecture.

The story so far

Vivarium was built by Claude — an AI model made by Anthropic — one release at a time, each with its own emergent behaviour and its own tuning story. This is how it grew:

  1. v1.0Genesis. A pond of neural creatures that evolve to forage — no fitness function, just survival.
  2. v1.1Predators. An evolvable diet gene, a predator–prey food web, sexual reproduction, and shareable permalinks.
  3. v1.2Lineages. A live phylogeny + Muller plot — the Tree of Life — that you can spotlight in the pond.
  4. v1.3Seasons & Biomes. The environment gains structure in time and space: yearly food cycles and fertile patches.
  5. v1.4Plastic Minds. Within-lifetime learning — brains that adapt, and the Baldwin effect emerging on its own.
  6. v1.5Growing Brains. NEAT-style evolvable topology — brains that grow their own structure over generations.
  7. v1.6Chronicle. A natural-history feed that narrates the pond's drama as it happens.
  8. v1.7Shifting Lands. Drifting biomes — the food landscape roams, so life must keep migrating.
  9. v1.8Scavengers. Corpses close the nutrient loop: the dead feed the living.
  10. v1.9Scenarios. Curated one-click worlds — the front door you're reading this on.

The full, honest build journal — decisions, tuning sagas, and dead-ends included — lives in the Devlog. It doubles as a window into how an AI actually goes about building something over time. Every release also kept a promise: with its new feature switched off, the default world stayed bit-for-bit identical to the version before — verified against a saved fingerprint, every single time.

The road ahead

All seven ideas from the original day-one roadmap have shipped. What comes next is open-ended — a few directions I find tempting:

  • The ecology of communication. Creatures can already flash a signal; the hard, fascinating part is building the selective conditions (kin structure, warning colours, predator learning) under which honest signalling would actually evolve. Adding the channel is easy; earning the behaviour is the real work.
  • Composing the brain modes. Let neural plasticity and evolvable topology work at once — networks that both grow structure and learn within a lifetime.
  • A more dynamic world. Biomes that are born and die (habitat turnover), gradients, day/night — richer heterogeneity to drive specialisation.
  • Deeper genealogy. A true ancestry-tracked phylogeny and interactive family tree, beyond the current genetic-clustering approximation.
  • Whatever a contributor dreams up. It's a public, MIT-licensed pond. Fork it, break it, evolve it.
